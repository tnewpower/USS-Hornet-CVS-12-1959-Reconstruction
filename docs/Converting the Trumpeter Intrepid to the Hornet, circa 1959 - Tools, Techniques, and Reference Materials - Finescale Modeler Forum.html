I’m doing a little bit of work on the Model Monkey island. I will probably treat it as a separate model and build it fully, paint it and have it ready to install on the flight deck as a complete unit. This should make doing the rigging, installing antennas, railings, etc. a bit easier. The Model Monkey island is excellent, but it is circa 1969, 10 years after my era, and there were some changes, most notably the SPN-6 approach radar from the 1959 cruise was replaced with the SPN-35. Both were mounted on the aft end of the island, but the SPN-35 sits inside a much larger domed housing than the SPN-6. So I cut off the SPN-35 housing and designed a smaller one that I will 3D print. Model Monkey also provides the SPN-6 in their catalog, so I purchased and will use that on my mount. There was also a semi-circular indent on the deck where the SPN-35 was mounted, so I trimmed off a bit of the original mount and fitted it into that recess. With some CA and accelerator, and a lot of sanding, I’ve got it ready to take the new mount. Patience is key when sawing the resin, as it is fully cured and more brittle as a result. I have the island cleaned up mostly, but have a little bit of filler sanding to do, then I’ll fit the new mount. The first photo shows the SPN-6, and the second the SPN-35. You can see how much bigger the -35 is.

Meanwhile, I just encountered my latest issue. The escalator that carried the pilots to the flight deck has a very prominent enclosure which is part of the superstructure on the starboard side, just below the island. Since the enclosure penetrates the flight deck and is also part of the island, getting the island and superstructure aligned is important, or you will recognize that something is off right away. When I aligned the island on the flight deck with the superstructure dry-fitted below, I realized that the enclosure needs to be about 7.5mm further forward than it was for the Intrepid kit. There’s a lot more detail in this part of the superstructure, so instead of trying to reprint the whole thing, I will first try and get just the section I need printed, and then graft it into the rest of the superstructure. I have designed this section already and am printing as this is being written. I also put a dummy enclosure into my Blender file to ensure the spacing of everything is good, so hopefully we will be in good shape with this print.

I decided that I needed to think through the assembly process, as I’m almost done with the major design changes to the kit, at least at the superstructure level, so I created this assembly guide to make sure I get things done in (what I hope is) the right order. Since my focus is now on getting the hull sorted before I paint the upper surfaces, today I printed a shim for the forward-most part of the bow. After some very careful work with the razor saw, sanding sponges, and files, I got the scars removed and the new sponson fitted. I might be the only one that notices these small details, but I feel good about getting it right.

Happy 4th and 250th to everyone! Looks like it was quite a show in NYC today; here in Colorado, we’re celebrating, but cautiously due to the fire danger. So, I’ve been doing more research today, and I learned something important. In my previous post, I mentioned doing the sponsons for the Hornet, as they differed from the Intrepid. Well, after looking at my 1959 cruise book, I noticed something, namely, that there were no visible sponsons on the starboard side, but instead it appeared that there were small platforms to assist with the UNREPs. This got me looking to confirm the layout and I went online to the great navsite.de page to consult the 1958 and 1957 Hornet Cruise Books. Lo and behold, I finally found what I was looking for in this great shot from the 1957 Cruise Book. Thank you, Supply Department. It’s clear in this shot that there were no sponsons at the deck level, but there is in fact a sponson of sorts under the island, just forward of the escalator enclosure. The 1957 cruise book has an awesome UNREP section, and I was able to get pictures of this command and control sponson from all angles. Knowing the rate of change and evolution of the Essex class during this time, just because it was there in 1957 did not mean it would be there in 1959. I did find compelling enough evidence of its presence in 1959 from that cruise book in a bow-on shot. So, I made reasonable guesstimates of the size of the sponson and am doing a test print now. I will also be removing the deck-level sponsons and instead will fit small platforms where the sponsons would be installed later, most likely during the 1961 refit at Puget Sound NSY. I have enjoyed the detective work to get this configuration right. There does not seem to be a definitive source for this configuration, so it’s up to me to create it!

Also, while starting to do more research on the island itself, I discovered a pretty major discrepancy between the Model Monkey Hornet island and how the island was configured in 1959. The Model Monkey island is for the Apollo 11 recovery era–1969–and it turns out there were some noticeable changes there, besides the SPN-6 being replaced by the SPN-35. And though I was able to make that change, there is a considerably more tricky difference on the forward portion of the tallest section of the island–namely, a large rectangular box that sits on that face for the 1969 version, but is not present in 1959. Given the prominence of this piece and the trickiness of trying to remove the box, I looked at the other Model Monkey offerings and determined that the USS Yorktown island for 1955-1960 is, as far as I can tell, a very accurate rendering for the Hornet in 1959, which makes sense. And, bonus, it comes with the SPN-6 already integrated, though it is missing the platform for the SPS-8 height finding radar that is present on the Hornet island. Not to worry, I designed and printed my own platform, along with the mast for the SPS-29 radar on the starboard side of the island. The kit has this in PE, but it will look 2D on the ship, so I opted for printing it as well. It’s a bit delicate, but I feel I should be able to get it in place without too much fiddling. I tried to print the railing around the radar antenna platform, but it was a bit too fine for my printer to replicate.
